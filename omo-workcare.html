5.4.2. A

  1. Annotated Resources
    • The search results do not explicitly categorize authors of care work books as "post-modern economists," but they highlight several contemporary economists and scholars who approach the care economy through narrative, philosophical, feminist, and critical lenses rather than traditional neoclassical models.
    • Tim Jackson is a prominent economist whose recent work, The Care Economy, shifts away from standard economic analysis toward a narrative-driven, philosophical approach. He argues that prosperity should be understood in terms of health rather than wealth and that the economy’s purpose should be care rather than growth. His writing blends economics with history, poetry, and mythology, reflecting a post-modern or post-growth sensibility that critiques capitalist growth paradigms.
    • Helma Lutz, a sociologist and former professor of Women’s and Gender Studies, authored The Backstage of the Care Economy (published May 2025). This work examines the commercialization of migrant care work and transnational motherhood, grounding its analysis in radical social thought and feminist critiques of capitalism. While sociological rather than strictly economic, it is cited as a key text in reshaping economic thinking on care and migration.
    • Premilla Nadasen, a historian, wrote Care, which traces the care economy’s roots in racial capitalism and slavery to the modern "care-industrial complex." Her work highlights how the exploitation of reproductive labor serves profit motives and emphasizes collective care and resistance by low-wage workers, immigrants, and women of color.
    • Other relevant perspectives include:
      • Feminist Economics: Scholars like Naila Razavi and Nancy Folbre are cited for establishing that unpaid care work is crucial to human wellbeing and for developing models like the care diamond to link market accumulation with social reproduction.
      • Ecological Economics: Researchers like Paola Barca and Lang discuss "Earthcare" and indigenous care ethics, linking care for the environment and community to social reproduction and climate resilience.
      • Health Care Reform: Economists Liran Einav and Amy Finkelstein propose a reboot of U.S. health care with universal basic coverage, focusing on systemic efficiency rather than traditional care economy theory.
    • These works collectively represent a shift in economic thought toward care, sustainability, and social reproduction, often employing interdisciplinary methods that challenge conventional economic assumptions.
